    [4 marks]

    Given \(w = 2 + 2\mathrm{i}\) and \(z = \cos\dfrac{5\pi}{6} + \mathrm{i}\sin\dfrac{5\pi}{6}\), find in its simplest form \(w^4z^6\)

    When dealing with multiplication, convert complex numbers to Euler's form. For \(w\), the modulus, \(r = \sqrt{8}\) and the argument, \(\theta = \dfrac{\pi}{4}\).

    For \(z\), \(r = 1\) and \(\theta = \dfrac{5\pi}{6}\).

    \(w^4 = \left(\sqrt{8}e^{\mathrm{i}\frac{\pi}{4}}\right)^4 = 64e^{\mathrm{i}\pi}\).

    \(z^6 = \left(1e^{\mathrm{i}\frac{5\pi}{6}}\right)^6 = e^{\mathrm{i}\times5\pi}\).

    \(w^4z^6 = 64e^{\pi\mathrm{i} + 5\pi\mathrm{i}} = 64e^{6\pi\mathrm{i}} = 64\).

4. Vectors

  • 4.1

    • Concept of a vector.
    • Representation of vectors using directed line segments.
    • Unit vectors; base vectors \(\textbf{i}, \textbf{j}, \textbf{k}\).
    • Components of a vector: \(\textbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}v_1\\v_2\\v_3\end{pmatrix} = v_1\textbf{i} + v_2\textbf{j} + v_3\textbf{k}\).
    • Algebraic and geometric approaches to the following:
      • the sum and difference of two vectors;
      • the zero vector \(\textbf{0}\), the vector \(-\textbf{v}\);
      • multiplication by a scalar, \(k\textbf{v}\);
      • magnitude of a vector, \(|\textbf{v}|\);
      • position vectors \(\overrightarrow{\mathrm{OA}} = \textbf{a}\)
      • \(\overrightarrow{\mathrm{AB}} = \textbf{b} - \textbf{a}\)
    • Proofs of geometrical properties using vectors.
    • Distance between points \(\mathrm{A}\) and \(\mathrm{B}\) is the magnitude \(\overrightarrow{\mathrm{AB}}\)

    Points \(A\) and \(B\) have the position vectors \(\textbf{a}\) and \(\textbf{b}\). Find \(|\textbf{a}-\textbf{b}|\) given \(|\textbf{a}|=3\), \(|\textbf{b}|=4\), \(\phi=\dfrac{\pi}{3}\).

    Remember that \(|\textbf{a}-\textbf{b}|\) is the vector going from \(B\) to \(A\) i.e. \(\overrightarrow{\mathrm{BA}}\).

    \(|\textbf{a}|\) is the length of the vector \(\textbf{a}\).

    This means we have the lengths of two sides of a triangle and the angle between them.

    We can solve using the cosine rune. If \(|\textbf{a}| = a\), \(|\textbf{b}| = b\) and \(|\textbf{a}-\textbf{b}| = c\), then \(c^2=a^2+b^2-2ab\cos\phi\) and \(c=3.61\).

  • 4.2

    • The definition of the scalar product of two vectors.
    • Properties of the scalar product:
      • \(\textbf{v}\cdot\textbf{w} = \textbf{w}\cdot\textbf{v}\)
      • \(\textbf{u}\cdot(\textbf{v}+\textbf{w}) = \textbf{u}\cdot\textbf{v} + \textbf{u}\cdot\textbf{w}\)
      • \((k\textbf{v})\cdot\textbf{w} = k(\textbf{v}\cdot\textbf{w})\)
      • \(\textbf{v}\cdot\textbf{v} = |v|^2\)
    • The angle between two vectors.
    • Perpendicular vectors; parallel vectors.
    • \(\textbf{v}\cdot\textbf{w} = |\textbf{v}||\textbf{w}|\cos\theta\) where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\textbf{v}\) and \(\textbf{w}\).
    • Link to 3.6.
    • For non-zero vectors, \(\textbf{v}\cdot\textbf{w} = 0\) is equivalent to the vectors being perpendicular.
    • For parallel vectors, \(|\textbf{v}\cdot\textbf{w}| = |\textbf{v}||\textbf{w}|\)

    Points \(A\) and \(B\) have the position vectors \(\textbf{a}\) and \(\textbf{b}\) with respect to the origin \(O\). Find the angle \(AOB\) given \(|\textbf{a}|=3\), \(|\textbf{b}|=4\) and \(\textbf{a}\cdot\textbf{b}=6\sqrt{2}\).

    Remember the definition of the scalar/dot product: \(\textbf{a}\cdot\textbf{b}=|\textbf{a}||\textbf{b}|\cos\theta\).

    We are looking for the angle, so we can rearrange this to give \(\cos\theta = \dfrac{\textbf{a}\cdot\textbf{b}}{|\textbf{a}||\textbf{b}|}\).

    Putting the numbers in we get \(\cos\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \Rightarrow \theta = \dfrac{\pi}{2}\)

    Points \(A\) and \(B\) have position vectors relative to the origin \(O\) given by

    \(\overrightarrow{\mathrm{OA}} = \begin{pmatrix}3\sin\alpha\\2\cos\alpha\\-1\end{pmatrix}\) and \(\overrightarrow{\mathrm{OB}} = \begin{pmatrix}2\cos\alpha\\4\sin\alpha\\3\end{pmatrix}\)

    where \(0^{\circ} < \alpha < 90^{\circ}\) and \(\overrightarrow{\mathrm{OA}}\) and \(\overrightarrow{\mathrm{OB}}\) are perpendicular. Find \(\alpha\).

    Since \(\overrightarrow{\mathrm{OA}}\) and \(\overrightarrow{\mathrm{OB}}\) are perpendicular, we know the angle between the two vectors is \(90^{\circ}\). \(\cos 90^{\circ} = 0\) so their dot product is zero.

    \(\begin{pmatrix}3\sin\alpha\\2\cos\alpha\\-1\end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix}2\cos\alpha\\4\sin\alpha\\3\end{pmatrix} = 0\)

    \(3\sin\alpha\times2\cos\alpha + 2\cos\alpha\times4\sin\alpha + (-1)\times 3 = 0\)

    \(14\sin\alpha\cos\alpha = 3\).

    Using the trigonometric identity \(\sin 2\theta = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta\) gives:

    \(7\sin 2\alpha = 3\).

    \(2\alpha = \sin^{-1} \frac{3}{7} \Rightarrow \alpha = 0.22\) and \(2\alpha = \pi - \sin^{-1} \frac{3}{7} \Rightarrow \alpha = 1.35\).

  • 4.3

    • Vector equation of a line in two and three dimensions: \(\textbf{r} = \textbf{a}+\lambda\textbf{b}\).
    • Simple applications to kinematics.
    • The angle between two lines.
    • Knowledge of the following forms for equations of lines.
    • Parametric form: \(x = x_0 + \lambda l\), \(y = y_0 + \lambda m\), \(z = z_0 + \lambda n\).
    • Cartesian form: \(\dfrac{x-x_0}{l} = \dfrac{y-y_0}{m} = \dfrac{z-z_0}{n}\)

    Find the acute angle between the lines \(l_1: \dfrac{x-3}{2}=\dfrac{y+2}{-1}=\dfrac{z}{\sqrt{3}}\) and \(l_2: \dfrac{x+1}{1}=\dfrac{y-2}{1}=\dfrac{z-1}{\sqrt{3}}\).

    Begin by converting the equations into vector form:

    For \(l_1\): \(\dfrac{x-3}{2}=\lambda \Rightarrow x = 3 + 2\lambda\), \(\dfrac{y+2}{-1} = \lambda \Rightarrow y = -2 -\lambda\) and \(\dfrac{z}{\sqrt{3}} = \lambda \Rightarrow z = \sqrt{3}\lambda\).

    The vector equation of the lines:

    \(l_1: \begin{pmatrix}3\\-2\\0\end{pmatrix} + \lambda\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\\sqrt{3}\end{pmatrix}\), \(l_2: \begin{pmatrix}-1\\2\\1\end{pmatrix} + \lambda\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\\\sqrt{3}\end{pmatrix}\).

    The direction of the lines are the vectors that are being multiplied by \(\lambda\) i.e. \(\textbf{d}_1=\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\\sqrt{3}\end{pmatrix}\) and \(\textbf{d}_2=\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\\\sqrt{3}\end{pmatrix}\).

    To find the angle we can use the scalar/dot product. \(\textbf{d}_1\cdot\textbf{d}_2 = 4\).

    This is also equal to \(\textbf{d}_1\cdot\textbf{d}_2 = \sqrt{2^1+(-1)^2+\sqrt{3}^2}\sqrt{1^2+1^2+\sqrt{3}^2}\cos\theta\).

    Rearranging gives \(\cos\theta = \dfrac{4}{\sqrt{8}{\sqrt{5}}}\) and \(\theta = 0.89 = 50.8^{\circ}\).

  • 4.4

    • Coincident, parallel, intersecting and skew lines; distinguishing between these cases.
    • Points of intersection.

    \(l_1\) and \(l_2\) are two lines where \(l_1: \begin{pmatrix}1\\-2\\4\end{pmatrix} + \lambda\begin{pmatrix}5\\1\\-3\end{pmatrix}\) and \(l_2: \begin{pmatrix}17\\4\\5\end{pmatrix} + \lambda\begin{pmatrix}6\\4\\7\end{pmatrix}\).

    Do the lines intersect? If they do, where do they interesct?

    In order for two lines to intersect, there must be a point which lies on both lines. Since the points that lie on each line are given by their equations, we can equate them.

    \(\begin{pmatrix}1\\-2\\4\end{pmatrix} + \lambda\begin{pmatrix}5\\1\\-3\end{pmatrix}\) = \(\begin{pmatrix}17\\4\\5\end{pmatrix} + \mu\begin{pmatrix}6\\4\\7\end{pmatrix}\).

    Where we changed one of the \(\lambda\) variables to a different letter since their values are independent.

    At the point where they meet, the x-coordinates must be the same i.e. \(1+5\lambda = 17 + 6\mu\). This is also true for the y- and z-coordinates: \(-2+\lambda = 4 + 4\mu\) and \(4-3\lambda = 5 + 7\mu\).

    Solving the first two equations simultaneously gives \(\lambda = 2,\ \mu = -1\). Substituting into the third equation gives \(-2\) on both sides of the equation. This shows that there is a value of \(\lambda\) and \(\mu\) which allows the lines to go through the same point, so they intersect.

    To find the coordinates of this point, we can use either equation of the line with the correct value of \(\lambda\) or \(\mu\) to get \((11,0,-2)\).

  • 4.5

    • The definition of the vector product of two vectors.
    • Properties of the vector product:
      • \(\textbf{v}\times\textbf{w} = -\textbf{w}\times\textbf{v}\)
      • \(\textbf{u}\times(\textbf{v}+\textbf{w}) = \textbf{u}\times\textbf{v} + \textbf{u}\times\textbf{w}\)
      • \((k\textbf{v})\times\textbf{w} = k(\textbf{v}\times\textbf{w})\)
      • \(\textbf{v}\times\textbf{v}=0\)
    • Geometric interpretation of \(|\textbf{v}\times\textbf{w}|\)
    • \(\textbf{v}\times\textbf{w} = |\textbf{v}||\textbf{w}|\sin\theta \hat{\textbf{n}}\) where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\textbf{v}\) and \(\textbf{w}\) and \(\hat{\textbf{n}}\) is the unit normal vector whose direction is given by the right hand screw rule.
    • Areas of triangles and parallelograms.
    [4 marks + 2 marks]

    Consider the points \(\mathrm{A}(1,2,3)\), \(\mathrm{B}(1,0,5)\), and \(\mathrm{C}(2,-1,4)\).

    a) Find \(\overrightarrow{\mathrm{AB}}\times\overrightarrow{\mathrm{AC}}\).

    b) Hence find the area of the triangle \(\mathrm{ABC}\).

    \(\overrightarrow{\mathrm{AB}} = (1,0,5) - (1,2,3) = (0, -2, 2)\).

    \(\overrightarrow{\mathrm{AC}} = (2,-1,4) - (1,2,3) = (1, -3, 1)\).

    \(\overrightarrow{\mathrm{AB}}\times\overrightarrow{\mathrm{AC}} = \begin{vmatrix}\textbf{i} & \textbf{j} & \textbf{k}\\ 0 & -2 & 2\\ 1 & -3 & 1\end{vmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}4\\2\\2\end{pmatrix}\).

    Area of triangle \(= \dfrac{1}{2}\left|\overrightarrow{\mathrm{AB}}\times\overrightarrow{\mathrm{AC}}\right| = \dfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{24} = \sqrt{6}\).

    The probability density function of the random variable \(X\) is defined as

    \(f(x) = \{ \begin{matrix}\sin x, & 0 \leq x \leq \frac{\pi}{2}\\ 0, & \mathrm{otherwise}.\end{matrix}\)

    Find \(\mathrm{E}(X)\).
